\section{Design Parameters \& Tool Setup}

\subsection{Parameters}
The NMOS and PMOS transistors are modeled using the typical values for 180nm CMOS Small-Signal Model\cite{mos_parameters}.
The SPICE directives for the NMOS and PMOS transistors are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NMOS:} \texttt{.model NMOS\_180nm NMOS (LEVEL=1 VTO=0.8 KP=90u GAMMA=0.5 PHI=0.68 LAMBDA=0.06 TOX=0.02u CJ=2.4e-4 CJSW=2.0e-10 CGSO=2.0e-10 CGDO=2.0e-10 PB=0.9 NSUB=6e15)}
    \item \textbf{PMOS:} \texttt{.model PMOS\_180nm PMOS (LEVEL=1 VTO=-0.9 KP=30u GAMMA=0.5 PHI=0.68 LAMBDA=0.06 TOX=0.02u CJ=2.4e-4 CJSW=2.0e-10 CGSO=2.0e-10 CGDO=2.0e-10 PB=0.9 NSUB=6e15)}
\end{itemize}

the output voltage (Z) is connected to the junction between the two transistors.

\section{DC Analysis (Voltage Transfer Characteristics)}
\subsection{VTC Curve Generation}
To obtain the Voltage Transfer Characteristic (VTC), a DC sweep simulation is performed, sweeping the input voltage from 0V to 1.8V with a step size of 0.001.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{vtc_curve.png}
    \caption{Voltage Transfer Characteristic (VTC) of the CMOS Inverter.}
    \label{fig:vtc_curve}
\end{figure}

From Fig.\ref{fig:vtc_curve}, the relationship between the Va and Vz is shown. 
It was found that when Va was lower than 0.8V, the output voltage (Vz) was high, and 
when Va was higher than 0.9V, Vz was low.

\subsection{Critical Voltages}
From the VTC curve, the critical voltages are extracted (where the slope is -1):
\begin{itemize}
    \item $V_{OH} = $ 1.8 V
    \item $V_{OL} = $ 0 V
    \item $V_{IH} = $ 0.857 V
    \item $V_{IL} = $ 0.829 V
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Noise Margins}
The noise margins are calculated using the extracted critical voltages:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High Noise Margin ($NM_H = V_{OH} - V_{IH}$) = 0.943 V
    \item Low Noise Margin ($NM_L = V_{IL} - V_{OL}$) = 0.829 V
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Switching Threshold}
The switching threshold ($V_M$), where $V_{in} = V_{out}$, is measured from the VTC:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $V_M = $ 0.845 V
\end{itemize}

\section{Transient Analysis (Timing Characteristics)}
\subsection{Transient Simulation Setup}
To analyze the timing characteristics, a transient simulation is performed. 
The input is driven by a pulse voltage source 
with the following parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $V_{initial} = $ 0 V, $V_{on} = $ 1.8 V
    \item $T_{delay} = $ 0 s, $T_{rise} = $ 1 ns, $T_{fall} = $ 1 ns
    \item $T_{on} = $ 10 ns, $T_{period} = $ 20 ns
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Propagation Delay and Timing}
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{transient_waveform.png}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{transient_waveform_rise.png}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{transient_waveform_fall.png}
    \caption{Top: Transient response of the CMOS inverter. 
    Middle: Zoomed-in view of the rise time. 
    Bottom: Zoomed-in view of the fall time.}
    \label{fig:transient}
\end{figure}
The propagation delay is measured between the 50\% points of the input and output transitions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High-to-Low Delay ($t_{pHL}$) = 7.5 ps
    \item Low-to-High Delay ($t_{pLH}$) = 150 ps
    \item Average Delay ($t_p$) = 79 ps
\end{itemize}

The rise and fall times are measured between the 10\% and 90\% points of the output signal:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Rise Time ($t_r$) = 74 ps
    \item Fall Time ($t_f$) = 72 ps
\end{itemize}
